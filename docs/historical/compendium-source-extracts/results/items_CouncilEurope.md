# Council of Europe 2017 — Legal Regulation of Lobbying Activities (Recommendation CM/Rec(2017)2)

Source extract for the lobbying-disclosure compendium. Companion file: `items_CouncilEurope.tsv`.

## 1. Paper

**Citation.** Council of Europe, Committee of Ministers (2017). *Legal regulation of lobbying activities in the context of public decision making.* Recommendation CM/Rec(2017)2 and explanatory memorandum, adopted at the 1282nd meeting of the Ministers' Deputies on 22 March 2017. Strasbourg: Council of Europe Publishing. ISBN 978-92-871-8439-9. Available at https://rm.coe.int/legal-regulation-of-lobbying-activities/168073ed69.

**Framing.** This is a non-binding **recommendation** from the Committee of Ministers to the 47 Council of Europe member States. It is *not* an applied scorecard — it does not score jurisdictions on a numeric scale. The instrument's stated purpose is to "establish or further strengthen … a coherent and comprehensive framework for the legal regulation of lobbying activities" by setting out "guiding principles on devising policy at national level to regulate lobbying" (lines 142-156). The premise frames lobbying as "a legitimate, and certainly not an illicit, activity … an important and legitimate part of the democratic process" (lines 300-303); the regulatory aim is *transparency*, not suppression. Member States retain discretion over implementation in line with national circumstances.

The document has two parts: the **Recommendation proper** with a short Appendix containing 20 numbered "Guiding principles" grouped under 11 headings (Definitions + sections A–J), and a longer **Explanatory memorandum** that elaborates each principle with country examples. The principles are the load-bearing items; the memorandum provides interpretive context and country practice but introduces no additional normative items.

## 2. Methodology

How the Council of Europe constructed the principles (per paras 8-9, lines 435-468):

- The recommendation was drafted by the **European Committee on Legal Co-operation (CDCJ)** at the request of the Committee of Ministers.
- **Written consultation of over 500 persons** across 24 Council of Europe member States, drawing on Transparency International national branches, lobbyists, trade unions and industry confederations, academics, regulatory authorities and civil society NGOs.
- **One-day stakeholder hearing in Strasbourg on 14 June 2016** with 12 key actors from civil society, business, lobbyists and public bodies.
- The Committee of Ministers responds to a prior **Parliamentary Assembly call** (Recommendation 1908 (2010) on lobbying in a democratic society and Resolution 1744 (2010) on extra-institutional actors; Recommendation 2019 (2013) on corruption as a threat to the rule of law) for a "European code of good conduct on lobbying" (lines 119-125, 426-433).
- Prior CoE instruments referenced: **Criminal Law Convention on Corruption (ETS No. 173)**, **Civil Law Convention on Corruption (ETS No. 174)**, **Recommendation Rec(2000)10** on codes of conduct for public officials, **GRECO** monitoring work, and **Convention ETS No. 108** on automatic processing of personal data (lines 111-118).
- The recommendation explicitly **complements the OECD Principles for Transparency and Integrity in Lobbying** (lines 401-424), citing OECD's level-playing-field principle and rules-and-guidelines principle.

There is no formal scoring methodology — the instrument is a set of recommendations, not a measurement framework.

## 4. Indicator count and atomization decisions

**Total items captured: 41.** This is one fewer than FOCAL's claimed 42; see judgment calls below.

Atomization rules I applied:

- **Definitions**: each lettered definition (a-e) is one item. They are *definitional*, not regulatory — they set scope but do not impose obligations. I retained them because the project may want to know how CoE bounds "lobbying," "lobbyist," "public decision making," "public official," and "legal regulation."
- **Numbered principles with no sub-items** (P1, P3, P5, P6, P7, P8, P9, P10, P12, P13, P15, P16, P18, P20): one item each.
- **Numbered principles with lettered sub-items** (P2, P4, P11, P14, P17, P19): each lettered sub-item is its own item. For P14, I additionally captured the chapeau ("openness, transparency, honesty and integrity") because it states four substantive ethical principles distinct from the four behavioural expectations a-d. For P17, I retained P16 as the umbrella "appropriate measures" item and atomized P17.a (cooling-off period) and P17.b (guidance) — splitting P17.b's five bulleted guidance topics (gifts, communications, violations, COI, confidentiality) since each is a substantive guidance domain.
- **Indicator type**: almost all CoE items are coded `open` because they recommend that something *should* be done without specifying a measurable threshold. Three items are coded `categorical` because CoE specifies concrete attributes that admit yes/no checking: P9 (online + searchable + open + free), P11.a-c (the three minimum register fields), and P15 (sanctions must be effective + proportionate + dissuasive).
- **Scoring rule**: empty for all items. CoE is a recommendations document — it does not assign points or grade jurisdictions. Numeric examples in the explanatory memorandum (e.g., €60 000 fines in Austria, 5-year cooling-off in Canada federal) are illustrative country practice, not CoE-prescribed thresholds.

**Quirks in atomization:**

- P13 is structured as an *exception* to P7 (the register requirement may be satisfied by alternative open-government mechanisms). It is captured as one item but functionally negates the obligatory force of P7 in some jurisdictions.
- P12 ("registers may include additional information") is permissive, not mandatory. I captured it as one item but the project may want to flag it as soft.
- The sub-bullets under P17.b are guidance topics, not obligations on the lobbyist — they are obligations on the *public official's* training. The project may want to tag them with a different actor.

## 7. Notable quirks / open questions

- **Hard vs soft items.** Most CoE items are *soft* — phrased as "should" with member-State discretion on implementation. Only three items have *hard* concrete attributes that admit yes/no verification (P9 register accessibility quartet; P11 minimum register fields; P15 sanctions triad). Even P15's "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" triad is itself qualitative.
- **Permissive vs prescriptive.** P12 ("registers *may* include additional information"), P13 (alternative mechanisms can substitute for a register), and P17 ("could include cooling-off period") are explicitly permissive. The project may want to weight prescriptive ("should") and permissive ("may"/"could") items differently.
- **Jurisdictional scope.** Addressed to **47 Council of Europe member States** — primarily but not exclusively EU. Includes UK, Norway, Switzerland, Russia (at time of adoption), Turkey, Ukraine, all Western Balkans, etc. The recommendation is **non-binding** ("Recommends that governments of member States…"). Member States retain implementation discretion under national constitutional law.
- **Relevance to US state lobbying.** The CoE framework is **outside the US jurisdiction** but designed to be portable across diverse political systems. Many CoE country examples in the explanatory memorandum reference US federal practice (LDA semi-annual reports, Senate/House registration, $50 000 fines, 5-year DC criminal exposure) as comparators. The 41 atomized items map cleanly to dimensions used by US-focused frameworks (registration coverage, register accessibility, disclosure fields, ethics/COI, revolving-door/cooling-off, sanctions, oversight body), making CoE useful as a *crosswalk anchor* for project compendium work.
- **Definitions vs. regulatory items.** The 5 definitions (a-e) are not regulatory obligations. The project may want to filter them out when building a comparative scoring matrix, or retain them as scope-bounding context. I captured them because the source atomizes them as discrete items in the appendix.
- **Bidirectional revolving door.** P17.a covers *both* directions (public official → lobbyist *and* lobbyist → public official). Many US frameworks only score one direction.
- **Actor mismatch in P17.b.** P17.b's five guidance topics are obligations on *public officials* (training/conduct), not on *lobbyists*. The project's compendium may want a clear actor-tag column.
- **No quantitative thresholds in the principles themselves.** All numeric examples (cooling-off lengths, fines, percentages) appear only in the explanatory memorandum as country practice. The principles never prescribe a specific number.
