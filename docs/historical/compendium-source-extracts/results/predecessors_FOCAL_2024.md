# Predecessor citations — FOCAL (Lacy-Nichols et al. 2024)

Extracted by an isolated agent reading only `papers/text/Lacy_Nichols_2024__focal_scoping_review.txt` and `papers/Lacy-Nichols-Supple-File-1-IJHPM.pdf`. No comparison to PRI, no use of derived artifacts. Verbatim quotes preserved with line refs.

## 1. Paper

**Lacy-Nichols J, Baradar H, Crosbie E, Cullerton K. Lobbying in the sunlight: a scoping review of frameworks to measure the accessibility of lobbying disclosures.** *International Journal of Health Policy and Management*. 2024;13:8497. doi:10.34172/ijhpm.8497

**Review scope.** FOCAL is itself the synthesis output of a systematic scoping review of "frameworks for measuring or evaluating lobbying transparency" across peer-reviewed and grey literature. Inclusion required that a document "[p]rovides original or updated framework/structured list/specific criteria of elements that should be disclosed/made transparent about lobbying" and address "lobbyist registers and/or open diaries" (Table 1, lines 415-451). The review explicitly *excluded* enforcement/compliance, sanctions, ethics/integrity laws, cooling-off requirements, and broader policy-process access from the FOCAL synthesis itself, though many of the reviewed predecessors covered those topics. After screening 1727 peer-reviewed + 184 grey records and assessing 230 full texts, 15 frameworks (6 peer-reviewed, 9 grey) survived to the included corpus. The paper claims to be "the first to comprehensively identify and analyse the range of scholarly and NGO-led frameworks to assess lobbying transparency and disclosure" (lines 1235-1237).

The supplementary file (`Lacy-Nichols-Supple-File-1-IJHPM.pdf`) contains only the database-search strategies and the list of 23 organisation websites searched (OECD, IDEA, US Center for Public Integrity, Australian Centre for Public Integrity, Australian Democracy Network, ALTER-EU, SOMO, OpenSecrets, CorpWatch, Corporate Accountability, CEO, InfluenceMap, Friends of the Earth, Multinationals Observatory, Transnational Institute, Transparency International, Sunlight Foundation, lobbyingtransparency.net, Open Knowledge Foundation, Access Info, US Right to Know, Public Citizen). It does **not** list any frameworks beyond those in Table 2 of the main paper.

## 2. Reviewed corpus size

> "we included 15 frameworks from six peer-reviewed and nine grey literature articles in our review" — line 14-15.

> "Between 1991 and 2022, 15 frameworks were published with a focus on lobbying transparency and disclosure. Six were from the peer-reviewed literature, six from NGOs, two from government, and one from a master's research project (supervised by the lobbying regulation expert Chari)." — lines 400-404.

PRISMA flow (lines 311-339): Records identified n=2535 (databases) + 184 (grey via 159 websites + 25 Google) → 1911 screened → 230 full-text assessed (225 + 5 from backward search) → 15 included (6 peer-reviewed studies + 9 grey literature reports).

## 4. Notable quirks / open questions

- **Sunlight Foundation does not appear as a *standalone* predecessor** in FOCAL's reviewed corpus. It appears (a) as a co-author/co-publisher of #7 (the 2015 *International Standards for Lobbying Regulation*, jointly with Access Info Europe, Open Knowledge, and Transparency International), and (b) as a website searched in the grey-literature pass (yielded 0 hits per supplementary file 1, p. 4). FOCAL does **not** cite a standalone Sunlight Foundation US-state lobbying scorecard. If the parent project expected one, that's a gap relative to what FOCAL reviewed.
- **OpenSecrets does not appear as a standalone *measurement framework*** either — only as a data-display org (#25, ref 49) and as a website searched (yielded 7 potentially relevant records, but none made the included 15). The user's task description named OpenSecrets as a possible predecessor; FOCAL did not treat it that way.
- **PRI (Pacific Research Institute) is item #4 in the included corpus** under "Clemens, Kaszton, Rufer, Sucheski 2010, Pacific Research Institute" — included here per task instructions that PRI may surface by name in the citation list.
- **No CPI Hired Guns split.** The user's task mentioned CPI Hired Guns as a candidate; FOCAL covers it as item #3, ref 33 (US Center for Public Integrity, 2007). Note there is also an *Australian* "Centre for Public Integrity" listed as a separate org in the supplementary website search (publicintegrity.org.au) — distinct entity, no framework attributed to it in FOCAL's included corpus.
- **Federal vs state coverage.** Of the 15 included frameworks, five are explicitly US-state-focused (Opheim 1991, Newmark 2005, CPI Hired Guns 2007, PRI 2010, Newmark 2017). None of the 15 is a federal-LDA-specific rubric — LaPira & Thomas 2020 (#18) is the closest federal-measurement citation but sits outside the included corpus.
- **Item-15 minor discrepancy.** Newmark 2005's row in Table 2 says "3" categories but lists four phrases ("Definitions; frequency of reporting requirements; prohibited activities; disclosure requirements"). Reproduced verbatim; this is FOCAL's text, not a transcription error.
- **Roth 2020 (#12)** is hosted on a Google Sites page maintained as a master's-project supplement under Chari's "Regulating Lobbying" site; access is fragile (URL could disappear). FOCAL provides only this single URL.
- **Two refs where FOCAL ref-numbers shift between body and Table 2.** I have used the reference-list numbering (lines 1289-1469) throughout. Ref 41 (the 2015 International Standards) is the framework reviewed; ref 1 is the 2022 update of the same standards used in the introduction. Both share the lobbyingtransparency.net domain.
- **Supplementary file added nothing new.** The supplementary PDF is search-strategy documentation only — no additional frameworks beyond Table 2.
- **The included corpus is 15, but the FOCAL ref list cites only 14 distinct refs for the 15 frameworks** because the *International Standards* document is referenced once (ref 41) yet authored jointly by four orgs. I have treated it as a single predecessor.
