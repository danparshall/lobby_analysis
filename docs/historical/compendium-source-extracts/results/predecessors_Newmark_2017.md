# Predecessor citations — Newmark 2017

Extraction of prior lobbying-regulation rubrics, measures, and measurement-bearing
empirical studies cited substantively in Newmark 2017. Source extracted only from
`papers/text/Newmark_2017__lobbying_regulation_revisited.txt`. No comparison to
or seeding from any other artifact.

## 1. Paper

**Newmark, Adam J. 2017.** "Lobbying regulation in the states revisited: What are
we trying to measure, and how do we measure it?" *Interest Groups & Advocacy*.
DOI: 10.1057/s41309-017-0023-z. (Macmillan Publishers Ltd 2017.)
Affiliation: Appalachian State University, Boone, NC.

**Comparison scope.** The paper explicitly revisits the universe of lobbying-
regulation measures used by political scientists and good-government groups, and
constructs an updated 2015 index it then correlates against four alternative,
contemporary measures (Sunlight Foundation, Center for Public Integrity, PRI
disclosure, PRI accessibility) plus Newmark's own 2003 Book-of-the-States-derived
measure. From the abstract (line 12-14): *"The result is a multitude of measures
that often assess very different constructs. This article creates an updated
measure of lobbying regulation and compares this measure to previous and current,
alternative measures."* The literature review (lines 46-83) traces the rubric
lineage from Opheim (1991) forward through Newmark (2005) and downstream studies
that adopted that measure, plus a parallel set of organizational rubrics
(BOS, NCSL, Sunlight, CPI, PRI) reviewed in the "Different means of assessment"
section (lines 147-202).

rubrics were compared." The empirical comparison in the results section is
against five alternative measures (the four shown in Table 1 plus the Newmark

## 4. Notable quirks / open questions

- **No explicit "N rubrics compared" number.** Newmark 2017 does not state a
  count for the predecessor universe. The empirical comparison in the results
  section is against five concrete measures (Sunlight, CPI, PRI-disclosure,
  PRI-accessibility, Newmark-2005/2003-BOS), but the literature review names
  several additional rubrics and downstream studies without enumeration. I have
  not invented a corpus number; section 2 captures what Newmark actually wrote.

- **CPI "Hired Guns" naming.** The user prompt references "CPI Hired Guns" — Newmark
  2017 does not use the phrase "Hired Guns" in the extracted text. He calls it
  the "CPI lobbying disclosure" measure within the broader "State Integrity
  Investigation" (lines 179-183, 489-492). The prompt's "Hired Guns" framing
  appears to be the user's own shorthand for the same instrument; I have noted
  the CPI rubric under its Newmark-verbatim labels.

- **Headline cross-rubric correlations** (these are Newmark's empirical findings
  about the predecessor corpus; included here because the task asks for them):
  - **PRI-disclosure vs. CPI-disclosure**: r = 0.04 (line 422). Verbatim:
    *"Surprisingly, even the disclosure measures constructed by CPI and PRI are
    unrelated (Pearson's r = 0.04). Despite some common components, these
    organizations actually measure disclosure in different ways."*
    (`papers/text/Newmark_2017__lobbying_regulation_revisited.txt:421-423`)
  - **PRI's two internal indices (disclosure vs. accessibility)**: r = 0.25
    (line 417). Verbatim: *"disclosure and accessibility to information are two
    different things, and the correlation between the two PRI rankings is only
    0.25."*
    (`papers/text/Newmark_2017__lobbying_regulation_revisited.txt:416-417`)
  - **New 2015 index vs. Newmark-2005/2003-BOS**: r = 0.54 (line 572).
  - **New 2015 index vs. Sunlight**: r = 0.4 (line 945).
  - **New 2015 index vs. CPI**: r = 0.52 (line 953).
  - **New 2015 index vs. PRI-accessibility**: r = 0.27 (line 955).
  - **New 2015 index vs. PRI-disclosure**: *"generally unrelated"* (line 956).

- **Borderline inclusions / judgment calls.**
  - *Witko 2005 / 2007* — campaign-finance-regulation indices, NOT lobbying
    regulation per se. Tagged `methodological-instrument` because Newmark
    explicitly uses them as relevant adjacent measurement work
    (lines 79-82): "campaign ﬁnance regulations restrict some lobbying-related
    activities." Included.
  - *Vaughan and Newmark 2008* and *Newmark and Vaughan 2014* — ethics-research
    pieces, used by Newmark to motivate the enforcement-side conceptual
    apparatus. Both are cited substantively (not glancingly), so retained.
  - *Rosenson 2005* — book on state ethics laws (adjacent regulatory regime).
    Cited substantively as an ethics-law parallel to lobbying regulation
    (lines 77-78); retained.
  - *LaPira and Thomas 2014* — cited only for the revolving-door definition
    (line 130-132). One short mention but it is part of the conceptual scaffolding
    for one of Newmark 2015's index components ("revolving door restrictions"),
    so retained.
  - *Hogan et al. 2008 / Mihut 2008 / Holman 2008* — cited together as a single
    cross-national cluster; each is named only briefly, but Newmark frames them
    as the corpus of *"recent works have focused on lobbying across nations"*
    (line 69), which is substantive enough for inclusion.

- **No theoretical-only citations were excluded** from this list — every entry
  here either constructs a measure, uses one, or is named by Newmark as part of
  his rubric-comparison universe.
