Policy Points:
r Our research provides a starting point to benchmark government transparency mea-
sures to improve the quality and accessibility of information about lobbying.
r Policymakers and regulators can use our framework to develop or strengthen their
own lobbying registers.
r Countries like Canada are a good example of how to design an accessible database
about lobbying, and Chile, Ireland, and Scotland provide examples of comprehensive
contact logs.
Context: Information about lobbying is crucial to alert the public about undue influence
in government decision making. Yet, government disclosures of lobbying activities are rare
internationally and vary considerably in their completeness and accessibility. Building on a
framework to measure lobbying transparency, this study benchmarked national government
disclosures to understand what information was shared and to develop recommendations to
strengthen political transparency.
Methods: To identify lobbyist registers, we reviewed four international surveys that assessed
lobbying transparency in 128 countries. For each country with an online register, we assessed
the 50 indicators in the Framework for Comprehensive and Accessible Lobbying (FOCAL),
generating an overall score for each government. To highlight the importance of transparency
for public health, we compared the visibility of tobacco industry lobbying across all registers

Findings: We identified 28 countries with online lobbyist registers, all from upper or upper-
middle income countries. No country fulfilled all 50 indicators in the FOCAL. The category of
“scope” had the highest scores across countries, whereas the “revolving door” and “financial”
categories had the lowest scores. We found evidence of lobbying by Philip Morris Interna-
tional, British American Tobacco, or one of their subsidiaries in 14 of the 28 countries with
online lobbyist registers.
Conclusions: Our study empirically demonstrates the hidden nature of lobbying interna-
tionally. In the case of industries whose interests conflict with public health, poor lobbying
transparency presents a risk that vested interests may undermine public health policymak-
ing without anyone knowing. To ensure that health harming industry interests cannot escape
scrutiny, public health advocates should support efforts to strengthen political transparency
measures.

M
any of the most egregious examples of commercial conflicts of
interest and undue influence in politics come from ad hoc or unconven-
tional sources. It was litigation against major tobacco companies such as
Philip Morris International (PMI) and R. J. Reynolds Tobacco Company that led to
the trove of documents released during discovery. In these papers, we can see internal
documents detailing company strategies to influence governments and block public
health policies. Examples include developing weaker voluntary regulation as policy
alternatives, cultivating relationships with influential stakeholders to lobby on their
behalf, and threatening legal action.1 Litigation against pharmaceutical and chemi-
cal companies have led to document disclosures revealing similar practices in other
industries.2–4
The Panama and Paradise papers published by the International Consortium of In-
vestigative Journalists not only revealed a global network of tax minimization (and
evasion) but also highlighted the politically well-connected world of the very wealthy
(e.g., high–net worth individuals). Investigative journalists uncovered how Purdue
Pharma provided political donations and campaign contributions to US congress-
people to influence regulations regarding prescription drugs such as OxyContin.5
Whistleblowers revealed how the fossil fuel industry lobbied US agencies to cast
doubt and delay action on climate change.6
These examples highlight that corporate political influence is extensive and a bar-
rier to public health policymaking. They also highlight that current regulations

around political transparency are woefully inadequate. In the absence of strong trans-
parency and disclosure laws for lobbying, political donations, and other forms of
political influence, commercial actors can cultivate and hide extensive access to po-
litical elites and influence government policymaking—lobbying in the shadows so
to speak. Of course, the importance of some commercial sectors to the economy (e.g.,
banking, telecommunication, energy) predisposes governments to make probusiness
decisions even in the absence of lobbying.7 Nonetheless, we focus here on the explic-
itly political strategies used by commercial actors to influence government decision
making as an important manifestation of corporate power and influence that should
be interrogated.
We note that lobbying itself is not inherently problematic. Indeed, civic partici-
pation in government is crucial to healthy democracies. Citizens, community groups,
nongovernmental organizations (NGOs), and other organizations engage with poli-
cymakers and make their voices and perspectives heard through lobbying, albeit this
lobbying is often labeled “advocacy” to conceptually separate it from more business-
driven lobbying agendas. The problem is that access to governments tends to be
imbalanced in favor of powerful interest groups. Political donations, for instance,
have been shown to facilitate access to politicians.8 Studies have shown that business
interests continually dominate lobbying efforts.9
Better transparency about who is meeting with policymakers, the interests they
represent, and the purpose of the meeting can provide crucial evidence about the
extent and nature of corporate political influence. Although transparency is not a
panacea for undue influence, it is an important first step toward generating aware-
ness about the extent to which some commercial actors may have an outsized in-
fluence on government decision making. Transparency can help to level the play-
ing field so that all actors understand who is lobbying governments. For public
health actors, better transparency could provide insights into the strategies used
by different industries to lobby governments.10 With this intelligence, health ad-
vocates could be better prepared to counter harmful industry interference in public
health policymaking. Better lobbying intelligence can also alert public health ac-
tors about potential conflicts of interest—for instance, if health organizations are
hiring tobacco lobbyists (a common scenario in the United States).11 Evidence of
undue influence could attract media attention, which could in turn help draw at-
tention to specific advocacy issues and aid advocates to pressure governments to re-
form their rules around lobbying. Ultimately, transparency is one of the core foun-
dations of accountability and trust in decision making that is essential to a healthy
democracy.12
Lobbyist registers are one of the ways that governments share information with
the public about lobbying. Registers vary in their scope and sophistication, with
some more akin to a directory of lobby firms and others providing linked data sets
and detailed records of lobbyist meetings.13 Registers likewise vary in their purpose,

with Holman and Luneburg14 arguing that some registers were designed to foster
transparency and public trust in government, whereas others were designed to provide
businesses with better access to policymakers, noting that the latter tended to be
weaker. There have been a few attempts to analyze the range of lobbying transparency
measures internationally. These have tended to focus more on the regulations and less
on the actual registers.15,16 One key exception is the Global Data Barometer, which,
in 2021, surveyed 109 countries about data governance and data ecosystems, with
political integrity as one of its key domains.17 They found 19 countries provided
lobbying data online.
Our project builds on these initial data sets, expanding the number of countries
analyzed and providing a deeper analysis of lobbyist registers by analyzing them di-
rectly. We were interested to know the following: What do they disclose? How do
they provide this information? What can governments learn from this to strengthen
or build their own transparency regimes? These questions complement analyses of
lobbying regulations that consider their compliance and enforcement (often lacking).
To measure and benchmark government lobbying disclosures, we analyzed existing
frameworks used to benchmark lobbying regulations18,19 to develop a Framework for
Comprehensive and Accessible Lobbying (FOCAL) comprising 50 indicators of best
practice lobbying disclosure.20 Here, we apply the FOCAL to measure actual govern-
ment disclosure practices. In addition to benchmarking governments, we also sought
to test how easy it was to implement and use the FOCAL. To highlight the public
health importance of transparency, we looked at a case study of tobacco industry lob-
bying. For each country with a lobbyist register, we explored whether it provided
information about tobacco industry lobbying and, if so, the nature of those data. This
gives us insights into the extent of tobacco industry lobbying in different countries
and highlights that some countries may be missing out on valuable intelligence be-
cause of weak disclosure regulations.
Methods
This study is the second stage of a project to measure lobbying transparency in-
ternationally. Here, we applied a framework (FOCAL) we designed to benchmark
government lobbying disclosure practices. We have focused on lobbyist registers,
as they are the commonest form of lobbying disclosure.17 Lobbyist registers docu-
ment the lobbyists and organizations involved in lobbying as well as (depending on
the jurisdiction’s requirements) their activities. Other similar forms of disclosure in-
clude open diaries or agendas (such as those in Argentina, the United Kingdom, or
some Australian states).15,21 These diaries provide a record of meetings between high-
level government officials and lobbyists (or other individuals and organizations) and
provide complementary information about the extent and nature of lobbying. Some

In the first stage of this study, we evaluated the transparency of government lob-

Stage 1. Benchmarking Government Lobbying Disclosures
Our first step was to identify governments with online lobbyist registers. We
used four existing international surveys of lobbying transparency: the Global Data
Barometer,17 the 2020 and 2023 surveys of lobbying regimes created by the French
High Authority for transparency in public life,22 and the Organisation for Economic
Co-operation and Development report Lobbying in the 21st Century: Transparency, In-
tegrity and Access.15 Data for these sources were collected between 2019 and 2023
(Israel’s data were collected in 2025, as the website was inaccessible prior to that).
Collectively, they assessed 128 countries. From these three data sets, we found only
28 countries with online lobbyist registers (Supplementary File 1 Table 1). For each
country, we sourced the policy, legislation, act, code, or other legal instrument that
underpinned the register (Supplementary File 1 Table 2). We also collected informa-
tion about each country’s World Bank region and income category. We chose to ex-
clude subnational registers (such as the Catalonia register or the Queensland register
in Australia), territories, and international organizations (e.g., the European Union).
We used Google Translate to convert policies and websites to English if no English
version was available. In some cases, an entire website could be converted to English,
whereas in others, text had to be copied from individual web pages. We note that the
language conversions may have some errors, and we flag this as a limitation in our
discussion.
In building our template to review lobbyist registers, we reviewed the 50 indicators
in the FOCAL, creating a set of notes to assign a value of yes, no, or partly for each of
the indicators, weighted two, one, and zero, respectively (Supplementary File 1 Table
3). Drawing on our analysis from the first phase of this project in which we developed
the FOCAL, we reviewed the three frameworks with weighted indicators and mapped
them against our 50 indicators (see Supplementary File 1 Table 4 for details).
To assess the transparency of each register, we first reviewed each country’s lob-
bying law (or other relevant legislation/act) to answer the scope and timeliness cat-
egories. At this stage, we combined two of the timeliness indicators (around up-
dating changes in general versus activities), as the answers were the same across all
registers.
To evaluate the other categories, we assessed the website hosting each lobbyist
register. Depending on the structure of the register, we reviewed the first three
pages (or ten entries) of the website or document. When relevant, we deliberately re-
viewed different types of entries (e.g., a professional vs. in-house lobbyist), as different

discussion.
Stage 2. Comparing the Visibility of Tobacco Industry Lobbying
In the second stage of this study, we sought to demonstrate the importance of lob-
bying transparency for public health by comparing the relative visibility of tobacco
industry lobbying. For each register, we performed a keyword search of the two largest
transnational tobacco companies (PMI and British American Tobacco [BAT]) as well
as variations of their names or affiliated and subsidiary companies (e.g., PMI, Philip
Morris, Altria, BAT, Benson, Rothman and Hedges, JP Carroll, Coltabaco). We note
that there are many other tobacco companies and names that could be searched (in-
cluding industry associations); however, for feasibility reasons, we limited our search
to two prominent and politically active companies. We note that in some countries,
they may not be the dominant tobacco companies, and we discuss this and other
limitations in our discussion.
For each register, we documented 1) if either company (or a subsidiary) was listed,
2) the information provided in the register about the companies (e.g., names of lobby
firms hired, names or number of lobbyists), 3) the number of reports or meetings
listed and the names of officials or departments contacted if the information was pro-
vided for the registers with contact logs, and 4) finally, we extracted the information
provided in contact logs to provide illustrative examples of the details provided in
some disclosures for the countries with the most detailed “topic” and “purpose” dis-
closures (Chile, Ireland, and Scotland). Supplementary File 2 details these searches.

only 22 of 83 possible high-income countries that had publicly accessible lobbyist
registers.
Lobbyist registers predominantly took one of two forms. Most common was a

Most countries placed the onus of responsibility for making disclosures on lobbyists
(even if the register was administered by a government department). However, in
three countries (Chile, Estonia, and Israel), government representatives were required
to make disclosures about their engagement with lobbyists. Of these, both Chile and
Estonia structured their lobbyist registers more as contact logs.
Some lobbyist registers provided interactive charts to share statistics about lob-
bying (such as number of registrants or meetings, types of organizations registers,
or the topics of meetings). From these, we were able to collect information about
the number of registrants (both companies and individuals), lobbyists, and clients
(companies or individuals that hired professional lobbyists) from most of the reg-
isters (Figure 2). When countries did not provide aggregate counts, in the case of
very small registers, we were able to manually count the entries; however, this was
not possible for large registers with thousands of entries (Chile, Estonia, and Peru). It
was immediately apparent that there was huge variability in the number of registered
lobbyists/organizations across countries, ranging from more than 26,000 lobbyists in
Germany to just 14 lobbyists in Montenegro.

Of the 28 countries fully assessed, none fulfilled all 50 indicators in the FOCAL
(Figure 3; see also Supplementary File 1 Table 5, which shows the unweighted bench-
marking). The category of “scope” had the highest scores across countries, whereas the
“revolving door” and “financial” categories had the lowest scores. For some countries,
it was not possible to assess all indicators, indicated by en dashes in the table. Below,
we discuss each category, highlighting some of the unusual cases we identified.
Scope. Overall, countries performed best in this category, with most having rela-
tively inclusive definitions of lobbyists, though this was less the case for government
targets and lobbying activities. Several countries limited registration requirements to
consultant lobbyists (Australia, Georgia, Greece, Lithuania, Poland, and the United
Kingdom). In other cases, only paid lobbying (i.e., excluding lobbying by volunteers
or private citizens) was included (in the case of Canada, Chile, Greece, Poland, Scot-
land, and the United Kingdom). The United States was the only country to impose
specific time and financial qualifiers on lobbyists (requiring registration only if more
than 20% of their time was spent on lobbying in a 3-month period and if expenses ex-
ceeded $3,000 USD for individuals and $13,000 for organizations). Some countries
excluded particular groups (e.g., Lithuania excluded NGOs and religious groups).
Other countries took an inclusive approach; for example, Estonia defined lobbyists as
follows23:
A lobbyist (including an advocate) is a natural or legal person who is engaged in
lobbying for interests, whether it is private, public or some social group’s interests,
for a fee or free of charge. For example, they following can be lobbyists: lawyers,
consultants, public relations specialists, etc; chambers of commerce and industry;
professional and business associations; trade unions; non-profit and interest associa-
tions; other organizations, associations and persons representing interests affecting
public life.
Several countries limited disclosure requirements to meetings or contact with specific
government representatives. For example, the United States excluded the executive
branch, Estonia excluded members of parliament, and Chile excluded meetings with
government staff.
Most countries had inclusive definitions of lobbying, with Scotland and France as
prominent outliers. Scotland required disclosures only for face-to-face meetings (ei-
ther in person or via a digital medium), whereas France only required disclosures for
meetings that were initiated by lobbyists. Most countries defined lobbying as some
form of direct contact (whether in person, over email, or at an event) with a gov-
ernment representative or staff member. Ireland, however, also included “grassroots”
lobbying, such as media campaigns to influence the general public.
We identified four countries with public ministerial diaries in which government
representatives documented their meetings with lobbyists, members of government,
or other individuals. In the case of Chile, diaries were embedded as part of the lobbyist
register, whereas in Estonia, Romania, and the United Kingdom, they were published

Timeliness. No country provided real-time disclosures, although Latvia had the most
robust timeliness requirements (2 days) for updating the register. Six countries re-
quired updates within 2 weeks (Australia, Chile, Colombia, Georgia, Lithuania, and
Poland).
Openness. All the countries that we analyzed had public lobbyist registers. Although
most countries had one lobbyist register, some had two or more, such as Estonia
(one for each department), Mexico (one each for the senate and chamber of deputies),
Poland (one for the upper and lower chambers of parliament as well as for the gov-
ernment), and the United States (the House and Senate each hosted a register, albeit
drawing on the same data). Although information was available online (our primary
inclusion criteria), the design of lobbyist registers often prevented a straightforward
search for a particular client or to see connections across clients or lobby firms (for
instance, a list of all registered lobbyists or clients for an organization). In some cases,
search functionality was limited or nonexistent. In Peru, for example, the register
could only be searched in 1-month blocks for specific names, making it challenging
to assess patterns of lobbying over time or to get a sense of the overall extent of lob-
bying. We reflect on this further in our analysis of tobacco industry lobbying below.
More than half of the registers allowed users to download data, although this often
excluded some data, such as historical lobbying. Only Canada, Finland, France, Mon-
tenegro, Scotland, and the United States allowed users to download the full register
in machine-readable format (such as a comma-separated value [CSV] or JavaScript
Object Notation [JSON] file) to support further analysis or data linkage (although
Ireland, Peru, and the United Kingdom also allowed users to download a limited
data set). Three countries (Canada, Slovenia, and the United States) provided unique
identifiers (IDs) for lobbyists and lobby firms/clients.
Descriptors. Beyond the full names of the registered organizations and/or lobbyists,
little information was provided to identify lobbyists. Poland was unique in that the
register provided for its lower chamber of parliament included photos of each lobby-
ist.
Revolving Door. Only four countries provided information about the revolving door
(Australia, Canada, Ireland, and the United States). Information varied across coun-
tries and individual disclosures. Ireland provided information about whether a lob-
byist was a former designated public official but did not provide dates of when they
exited office. The Australian register included information about the last position
held and the cessation date. Both Canada and the United States provided informa-
tion about multiple former government positions (albeit these data were not part of
the United States’ downloadable data set).
Relationships. Because of the organization of lobbyist registers, it was often chal-
lenging to get a sense of how different individuals or organizations were connected.

In Canada, for example, the focus on contact logs means that it is not possible to see a
lobby firm’s full list of lobbyists or clients (although this could be achieved through
linking and analyzing the downloadable data, this step requires a level of data lit-
eracy likely beyond that of the public). Only four countries provided aggregate lists
of clients (Australia, France, Germany, and the United Kingdom). In five registers,
companies disclosed a list of their membership or sponsorship of industry associa-
tions, though it was beyond the scope of this study to assess whether these lists were
complete (Austria, Finland, France, Germany, and Romania). We found no evidence
of the reverse, in which associations that lobby would list the members on whose be-
half they lobbied. Canada was the only register to disclose information about board
seats held by lobbyists (e.g., in associations or companies).
Financials. Only two countries (France and the United States) provided specific
information about the costs of lobbying, and in France, this was limited to total lob-
bying expenditure. The United States performed better than all other countries in
this category, providing a breakdown of income from specific clients as well as the
total lobbying expenses for organizations engaging in in-house lobbying. Canada and
Germany noted if companies or lobbying firms had received government funding (al-
though the amounts were undisclosed). Six countries (Australia, Austria, Belgium,
Canada, Colombia, and Germany) provided counts of the lobbyists employed, though
not their full-time equivalent (i.e., an indication of their lobbying workload). Chile
and the United States provided information about campaign contributions made by
lobbyists. In some cases, a country’s lobbying legislation required disclosure of finan-
cial information, but we could not find it. For instance, Georgia’s legislation requires
lobbyists to disclose money they received for lobbying, but we could not find evidence
of this in the register. We note that from 2026, Finland has committed to providing
information about the number of people engaged in lobbying, costs of outsourced
lobbying services, and other marketing costs.
Contact Log. Only eight countries provided contact logs with specific meeting dates
(Canada, Chile, Estonia, Greece Lithuania, Peru, Scotland, and Slovenia). Chile, Ire-
land, and Scotland provided the most information about the purpose of the meeting
(or other form of contact). In other cases, registers provided summary reports of con-
tact in aggregate form. In the United States, for example, organizations filed quar-
terly reports listing the departments contacted and the bills lobbied on. Although
Colombia did not have a contact log, it had the additional requirement that gov-
ernment representatives must provide information about trips funded by lobbyists
including the destination, cost, the organization funding it, and its purpose. We note
that Finland’s legislation commits to a detailed contact log; however, because of re-
cent implementation, it is too recent to view. More detailed examples of the level of
detail provided in each contact log are provided in the following section in which we
analyze tobacco industry lobbying.

Part 2. Comparing the Visibility of Tobacco Industry
Lobbying
We found evidence of tobacco industry lobbying (determined through the naming of
either PMI, BAT, or one of their subsidiaries in a lobbyist register) in 14 of the 28
countries with online lobbyist registers (Table 1). In Australia, PMI and BAT were
listed in the archives as previously hiring professional lobby firms but did not have
active lobbyists. Likewise, in Ireland, PMI was not actively lobbying (though contact
logs show they lobbied between 2016 and 2018). We note that we did not look
for tobacco trade associations or other tobacco companies, so the absence of tobacco
lobbying in our sample does not mean there was no tobacco lobbying through other
groups.
Depending on the country, different information was made available. Twelve of
the 14 countries with evidence of tobacco lobbying disclosed how many lobbyists
were working for the companies, with the United States listing the most lobbyists
(80 for PMI in 2023, including both in-house and consultant). As noted in the pre-
vious section, some countries, such as Australia and the United Kingdom, only re-
quired disclosure of consultant lobbyists, meaning that there may also be in-house
tobacco lobbyists that we could not identify. In Austria, France, Germany, and the
United States, we could see annual lobbying expenses, with PMI disclosing the high-
est amount ($3.17 million USD) in the United States in 2023. There was no informa-
tion about how much money was spent by PMI or BAT in the ten other countries they
were registered in. Six countries provided information about meetings, with Canada
recording the most meetings (244 for BAT between 2008 and 2024).
No country provided information for all three categories that we considered, and
some provided no information for any of the categories (such as Finland). Considering
the evidence we found in other countries, this raises questions about the nature of
lobbying that takes place in countries without records of meetings or expenses.
Chile, Ireland, and Scotland provided more detailed information about the purpose
of meetings (or other forms of communication) as well as the government representa-
tive they met with. From this, we were able to get a sense of each company’s regulatory
priorities and the frequency of its lobbying activities.
Of the 117 meetings held by BAT and PMI in Chile, 86 (74%) focused on the topic
of smuggling and the illicit cigarette market. However, beginning in October 2018,
PMI began to hold meetings to discuss “innovative” and “reduced-risk tobacco prod-
ucts.” In contrast, all 20 meetings in Scotland focused on the companies’ “smoke-free”
products and the development of regulations targeting vaping. Lobbying in Ireland
likewise focused strongly on vaping. However, it also addressed issues of tax, plain
packaging, and smuggling. Table 2 provides illustrative examples of the information
disclosed in the three registers.

Discussion
Of the 28 countries with publicly available, online lobbyist registers, none provided
complete transparency. Coupled with our analysis of tobacco industry lobbying by
PMI and BAT, this suggests that commercial actors who seek to undermine or block
the development of public health policies may be able to exert influence and hide
it. This reinforces our core concern and argument: public health advocates must pay
more attention to corporate political activity and support efforts to increase political
transparency to make it easier to see when, how, and why commercial actors (especially
those linked to harmful industries) engage in politics.
Our study builds on previous comparative analyses of lobbying regulation (see, for
instance, the work from Chari and colleagues comparing national regulations).16,24 In
contrast to previous studies that sought to evaluate lobbying regulations, we focused
on the practical question of disclosure (as opposed to enforcement mechanisms or
codes of conduct). Although the overall conclusion of our study is one of limited and
fragmented transparency (the sheer number of “0” scores in our framework indicates
that important features of transparency are frequently absent), we also found several
examples of good practices that countries seeking to reform and improve their lobby-
ing disclosures could emulate. For contact logs, Chile, Ireland, and Scotland provided
detailed information including of the topics and purpose of meetings, as well as the
specific government representatives that were contacted. Although Canada lacked
this level of detail, the design of its lobbyist register made it possible to analyze
meetings (and attendees) over time. This is important to get a sense of an industry
or company’s lobbying strategy over an extended period (such as who they met with,
which departments they focused on, or how frequently they sought to contact the
government).
The most consistent pattern across all countries we evaluated was the omission of
information. It is immensely telling that our highest scoring country (Canada) scored
less than 50%. In particular, most lobbyist registers had extremely limited informa-
tion about relationships between lobbyists (or companies engaged in lobbying) and
their government targets—a dynamic that raises concerns for conflicts of interest or
even corruption.25–27 Only four countries provided information about the revolving
door, and separate analyses of some of these disclosures suggest that they substantially
underrepresent the true extent of the revolving door.28,29 We found no countries that
required disclosures about direct business relationships between lobbyists or compa-
nies that lobby and public officials or their close family members (such as sharehold-
ings or employment). It may be that these data are available in other data sets (such as
gift registers or conflict of interest disclosures) that we did not analyze in this study.
On the one hand, the availability of data across multiple data sources highlights the
benefits that data linkage can offer in the realm of political transparency, in which

This leads us to a particular element of disclosure that has received limited atten-
tion in the literature to date: how the information is presented and made accessible.
The “openness” category of the FOCAL provides a number of indicators including
whether the data are machine readable (e.g., in structured format like an Excel table
vs. a list of lobbyists in a PDF) and whether the register is searchable (usually through
the aid of metadata, which categorizes and tags data [e.g., tagging whether a lobby-
ist has a revolving door profile or not, classifying the industry of companies, and, for
those with contact logs, classifying the government departments or levels in which
meetings occur]). Although seemingly quite technical, these elements are crucial for
ensuring that the data are easy to analyze and reuse. Although this was a challenge
within the 28 countries we evaluated, this issue is of course vastly compounded in
low-income countries, where not only is information about lobbying not available on-
line, but the data that do exist (e.g., about committee hearings or executive meetings
for implementing guidelines and regulations) are often disconnected and difficult to
search.30 The issue of disconnected data sets is especially obvious in countries with
multiple registers (e.g., Estonia, Mexico, United States). Although, on the one hand,
it is beneficial to have transparency over multiple levels and branches of government,
on the other hand, the fragmentation of data is an enormous barrier to easily making
sense of it.
If we compare our analysis of the Canadian register (with downloadable CSV files
that can be linked through unique IDs) to the UK register (with no downloadable
data), there was a stark difference in the speed with which we could explore the data
and identify patterns (for instance, whether companies are linked via the lobby firms
or lobbyists they employ). As a more general reflection, the task of applying the FO-
CAL to each country’s lobbying register was immensely time-consuming, requiring
in some cases searching and comparing multiple pages and lobbying entries to ensure
that variations in lobbying data presentation were documented as thoroughly as pos-
sible. For countries looking to improve the accessibility of their lobbyist registers, we
suggest the following two priorities: first, ensure data are downloadable in machine-
readable format (e.g., CVS), and second, use metadata to make the data searchable
and categorized. The Canadian register provides an excellent model for how this can
be done.
Comparing the performance of 28 countries helped us to identity outliers. For in-
stance, most countries used an inclusive definition of lobbyist that captured both
consultant and in-house lobbyists, as well as peak bodies, unions, and nonprofits
in other cases. However, six countries limited their registers to professional lobby-
ists only, rendering the remainder of their lobbying transparency effectively opaque.

Some jurisdictions (such as Australia) are seeking to reform this deficit in their leg-
islation, arguing that this not only allows many lobbyists to escape scrutiny but also
creates loopholes in other aspects of lobbying regulation.31 For instance, rules around
cooling-off periods to prevent conflicts of interest when government representatives
move into the private sector are based on definitions of lobbying. In Australia, a lim-
ited definition of “lobbyist” means that many former members of government have
moved directly into roles that other jurisdictions would define as lobbying without
any cooling-off period imposed.32 Reforms to expand the “scope” of lobbying legis-
lation would thus have important flow-on effects for other rules to protect against
conflicts of interest or corruption.33
Two other prominent outliers in our data were the United States and France for
their provisions of financial information. Despite many frameworks recommending
that lobbyist registers provide information about how much money was spent (or
received) on lobbying, this was extremely rare in practice.16,19 We also note that
the interest in financial information in frameworks evaluating lobbying regulations
reflects a US bias in the frameworks in which many were designed specifically to
evaluate US state disclosures (for which financial data are quite common).18,34 Our
study did not seek to understand or explain why some countries provided information
whereas others did not—in our section on future research, we suggest how this might
be explored.
A final crucial lacuna in lobbyist registers was information about meetings (or other
types of communication). Few countries provided contact logs, a major omission as
we view contact logs as one of the most imperative features of disclosure. Contact
logs allow us to see which members of government (or their staff) are being con-
tacted. They tell us about the volume of lobbying—does a group organize meetings
only a few times a year, or is there a dedicated campaign of frequent interactions? We
can see which specific individuals are involved in the communication, and coupled
with information about the revolving door, we can then see if there are any connec-
tions among the participants. Finally, and perhaps most importantly, a contact log
can provide details about the purpose of the meeting, as our analysis of 193 tobacco
industry meetings in Chile, Scotland, and Ireland showed. Analysis of contact logs
can provide insights about the issues companies or industries are concerned about as
well as the arguments they make.35 For instance, we were able to see a stark differ-
ence in strategies across the countries, as lobbying in Scotland had a singular focus
on “reduced-risk tobacco products” (e.g., vapes), whereas Chile had a much greater
focus on the topic of smuggling. These are common tobacco industry strategies to op-
pose electronic cigarette and tobacco standardized packaging proposals, which were
proposed in Scotland and Chile, respectively.36,37

shortcomings in lobbyist registers. For instance, we did not find any evidence in the

There are several limitations to our study. We looked only at one form of lobbying
disclosures (online lobbyist registers), whereas there are other data sets that provide
similar or complimentary information about lobbying. Argentina, for example, has
open diaries for the presidency but no lobbyist register. Other legal instruments that
provide transparency include Sunshine Principles, legislative footprints, conflict of
interest policies, public procurement registers, public agendas of policymakers, and
political donation registers.39 Likewise, our analysis is a snapshot in time. Countries
are continually updating and reforming their registers (such as Finland, which ef-
fectively overhauled its lobbying regulations during the course of this project). In
their analysis of political transparency, the Global Data Barometer flagged that some
countries had pledged to develop lobbyist registers (e.g., Taiwan and the Philippines),
though these had not progressed at the time of our study.17 Future iterations of our
project are likely to capture a larger set of countries or more robust disclosure mech-
anisms as countries continue to strengthen regulations.
A second limitation is that lobbyist registers were sometimes in languages other
than English, and we relied on Google Translate in most cases (E.C. is fluent in Span-
ish and was able to review the Chilean lobbyist registers). Although we are confident
the translations are robust, there may be nuances that are missed. It was also challeng-
ing to evaluate some indicators in the FOCAL because of the variations and discrep-
ancies within lobbying registers. In some cases in which registers had thousands of
entries, it was not feasible to assess the whole register, and the random sample we re-
viewed may not accurately reflect all entries. Likewise, it was sometimes impossible
to assess the completeness of client lists, and we may have missed some ambigu-
ous entries, such as those listed only as abbreviations, an issue previously flagged
by advocacy group Alliance for Lobbying Transparency and Ethics Regulation in
the European Union (ALTER-EU).41 Future studies could review a larger sample
of entries or apply data science tools to extract the complete data set for analysis,
which could help to answer questions about the level of compliance with lobbying
laws.
A third limitation concerns our analysis of tobacco industry lobbying. Our findings
of no lobbying in some countries may be a consequence of the political climate at the
time (e.g., a nonelection year). Further, PMI and BAT may not be the dominant
tobacco companies in some of the countries we analyzed, or they may lobby through
their trade associations or third parties instead of directly.

Lastly, an important caveat for transparency in general is that better disclosure
practices do not necessarily lead to lower levels of corruption or less corporate polit-
ical activity.42 The United States is a clear example where relatively strong political
transparency mechanisms exist comfortably (or uncomfortably, as the case may be)
alongside an environment of extensive corporate political activity and influence.43,44
To protect against undue influence, enforcement, sanctions, and a broader culture
of integrity are crucial elements that must sit alongside transparency mechanisms.33
The revolving door in particular highlights the increasing blurriness between govern-
ment and business interests and the importance of other measures to protect against
conflicts of interest, such as cooling-off periods or divestment of interests for public
office holders.
We would like to conclude by suggesting several avenues for further research aris-
ing from this study. Many lobby firms are part of larger corporate affairs companies,
in which public-facing, reputation management strategies are a core part of their
repertoire.45 A first avenue would be to explore the less direct forms of lobbying
that are captured in the Irish lobbying register. Although “grassroots” or “outside”
lobbying (i.e., mobilizing broad public pressure on governments) is often an advo-
cacy strategy for NGOs and nonprofits, it is also an important astroturf campaign
strategy (e.g., corporate advocacy masquerading as genuine grassroots).46,47 To what
extent do companies or lobby firms employ these strategies? A better understand-
ing of the range of influence strategies used by corporations can help public health
advocates anticipate and challenge undue political influence from harmful industries.
A further question for future research is understanding why lobbying regulation
has emerged in some jurisdictions as opposed to others. For instance, scholars have
used policy process theories (e.g., Multiple Streams Framework47 and Punctuated
Equilibrium Theory48) to analyze and explain why Germany reformed and strength-
ened its lobbying laws.49 Similar case studies could be applied in other countries to
deepen our understanding of the circumstances and opportunities that enable robust
transparency laws to be passed and enforced (including at the state or city level).
Similarly, we could explore whether lobbyist registers are predictive of other trans-
parency measures. Along similar lines, researchers could apply the FOCAL to state
governments in the United States, as many have more detailed disclosures than those
required by the federal government.
Lobbying data could also be mapped against policy timelines to get a sense for
how lobbying plays out against different policy or regulatory debates. What political
influence strategies are used (e.g., are particular members of government lobbied, or
do some commercial actors play a prominent role)? Our comparison of the different
arguments made in Chile, Scotland, and Ireland suggests that there were strategic
reasons for those tactics that could help us to understand (and better anticipate) future
lobbying activities. Previous studies of the tobacco industry have found a common
strategy is to lobby key leaders in government, such as presidents of committees,

For us, one of the next steps is to examine the practical utility of this framework
with policymakers and bureaucrats responsible for managing the registers to under-
stand the opportunities and barriers for implementing the level of detail and specifics
set out in the FOCAL. Do the weightings we assigned to categories resonate with
policymakers? Do they agree with our priority areas? And perhaps most importantly,
what do they see as politically feasible to implement, and how might that be achieved?
Conclusions
When lobbying occurs in the shadows, it prevents scrutiny, potentially giving rise
to conflicts of interest, undue influence, or even corruption. Transparency, although
not a panacea for these issues, is an important tool to minimize the risk of undue
influence. Our analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of existing national lobbyist
registers provides a starting point to benchmark government transparency measures.
Likewise, our snapshot of the visibility (or invisibility, as the case may be) of tobacco
industry lobbying highlights the urgency of strengthening existing lobbying regu-
lations. Beyond the lobbying registers analyzed in this study, we would like to point
readers to other avenues to increase the visibility of political influence, such as in-
vestigative journalism, freedom of information requests, whistle blower protections,
and litigation (in which the discovery process can reveal lobbying practices).19 We
hope that our work provides a useful tool for policymakers and advocates seeking to
improve lobbying transparency, as we strongly believe that political transparency is
a fundamental prerequisite for successful public health policymaking.
