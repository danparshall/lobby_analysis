Measuring State Legislative Lobbying Regulation, 
1990–2003
Adam J. Newmark, Appalachian State University
abstract
Since 1990, the American states have adopted a variety of reforms to regulate lob-
bying in an attempt to address a host of ethical issues. Such regulation can have 
important impacts on a variety of aspects of the legislative process because it typically 
impacts the relationships between legislators and lobbyists. How does such lobbying 
regulation vary across the states and over time? I have developed a measure of state 
lobbying regulation from 1990–2003. This measure validly captures the laws that 
limit relationships between lobbyists and legislators and, as such, can be used to help 
assess a variety of hypotheses about the legislative process.
States have long sought to govern the relationship between legisla-
tors and lobbyists through ethics laws, lobbyist registration, and reporting 
requirements (Rosenthal 2001). Lobbying regulations are designed to cur-
tail outright bribery and vote-buying but also to reduce the appearance of 
impropriety that reduces public trust in government. These regulations vary 
across states and time and may inﬂuence a variety of aspects of the legislative 
process. For example, South Carolina and Kentucky used to have lenient lob-
bying regulations but, following scandals over the past 10 years, these states 
now have extensive limitations on the relationship between lobbyists and 
legislators (Goodman, Holp, and Ludwig 1996; Ensign 1997).
 
Scholars’ ability to assess the impact of these laws has been hindered 
by the lack of a measure of lobbying regulation that is comparable across 
states and over time. Opheim (1991) assessed the variability of state lob-
bying regulation in the late 1980s with an index based on the deﬁnition 
of the term “lobbyist,” the extent of disclosure required, and the oversight 
and enforcement of regulations. Other researchers have since expanded our 
understanding of lobbying regulation, both the factors affecting it and how 
these inﬂuence lobbying communities, using ad hoc measures (Brinig, Hol-

In my study, I examine the underlying logic of lobbying regulation, focus-

I construct a new, replicable measure of lobbying regulation. After assessing 
the reliability and validity of the measure, I use it to show how lobbying 
regulation has changed over the past decade.
the purposes of lobbying regulation
The relationship between lobbyist and legislator has long been understood to 
be critical to the former’s inﬂuence in the legislative process and, indeed, to 
the functioning of the legislature itself (Rosenthal 2001). Thus, regulations 
that target this relationship should have the most potential for inﬂuence on 
the behavior and inﬂuence of lobbyists. I construct a replicable measure of 
lobbying regulation that is based on factors that inﬂuence the relationship 
between lobbyists and legislators, rather than one that includes all available 
indicators of lobbying restrictions and reporting requirements. For example, 
the expenditures that lobbyists make in performing their jobs would be 
relevant to this relationship, but how lobbyists themselves are compensated 
would not be.
 
One way to identify aspects of lobbying regulation that might affect the 
lobbyist-legislator relationship is to consider the purpose for which a given 
regulation is adopted. Some lobbying regulations are aimed at limiting which 
individuals and organizations can lobby in the state. While Brinig, Holcombe, 
and Schwartzstein (1993) argue that such regulations serve as barriers to 
entry into the profession by making lobbying more costly, others have found 
that such laws do not decrease the number of registered lobbyists in a state 
(Hunter, Wilson, and Brunk 1991; Lowery and Gray 1997), nor do they 
decrease the diversity of interest communities (Gray and Lowery 1998). The 
inconsistencies in these ﬁndings may, in part, be attributed to changes in state 
lobbying laws over the past decade or two. At the very least, a state’s deﬁni-
tion of the term “lobbyist” and the frequency with which they must register 
offer some indication of a state’s effort in regulating lobbying. Thus, a key 
component of lobbying regulation is how inclusive the state is in deﬁning 
who is a lobbyist.
 
A second type of lobbying regulation is designed to change systematically 
and directly the relationship between lobbyist and legislator. For example, lob-
byists regularly gave gifts to state legislators, took them to dinner, bought them

helped lobbyists ingratiate themselves with legislators, developing a relation-
ship that made it easier for the lobbyist to make his or her case to the legislator. 
Today, most states require that lobbyists report these gifts and some states 
ban them outright (Newmark 2003). States with stricter lobbying laws aim 
to prohibit a variety of behaviors, from giving monetary campaign contribu-
tions to buying gifts for legislators. At the extreme, lobbyists in some states 
are forbidden from providing legislators with so much as a cup of coffee.
 
Strict lobbying regulation typically requires the disclosure of various lob-
bying activities, gifts provided, and expenditures made while interacting with 
legislators. As important as any legal sanctions associated with these regula-
tions, legislators and lobbyists are likely just as concerned with the media’s 
and electoral opponent’s scrutiny that may follow large gifts or monetary 
contributions. This public scrutiny may regulate behavior because legisla-
tors do not want to be labeled as being in the pockets of special interests. Of 
course, legislators do accept legal campaign contributions from lobbyists and 
the groups they represent, but they may remain cautious when disclosure is 
required.
measuring lobbying regulation
The Book of the States (Council of State Governments, various years) provides 
data allowing us to assess states’ lobbying stringency by statutory deﬁnition, 
prohibited activities, and disclosure requirements (including the frequency 
of registration and reporting).1 This allows the construction of a measure of 
overall lobbying regulation in the states for six time periods: 1990–91, 1994–
95, 1996–97, 2000–01, 2002, and 2003.2 Some states have broader statutory 
deﬁnitions of what constitutes lobbying than others and this reﬂects stricter 
regulation. Similar to Opheim’s (1991) measure, the deﬁnitions component 
includes how many of the following seven criteria are deﬁned as lobbying in 
a state’s statute: legislative lobbying, administrative agency lobbying, elected 
ofﬁcials as lobbyists, public employees as lobbyists, and whether there is a 
compensation standard, expenditure standard, and time standard in the deﬁ-
nition of lobbying. Each of these components is coded 1 if the state considers 
a given behavior as lobbying and 0 otherwise. Additionally, more frequent 
lobbying disclosure and reporting requirements are an indication of more 
restrictive regulation. The frequency of reporting requirements was coded 1 
for those states that required registration and reporting more frequently than 
once per year and 0 for those states reporting once per year or less.

disclosure of certain activities, gifts received, or campaign contributions. 
Prohibited activities may include the following: campaign contributions at 
any time, campaign contributions during the legislative session, expenditures 
in excess of a certain dollar amount per year, and solicitation by ofﬁcials 
or employees for contributions or gifts. Disclosure requirements include 
the following six types: legislative/administrative action seeking to inﬂu-
ence, expenditures beneﬁting public ofﬁcials or employees, compensation 
received broken down by employers, total compensation received, categories 
of expenditures, and total expenditures. Again, each of these categories was 
coded 1 or 0 depending on whether the state had such a requirement. The 
values for all of these potential regulations were summed in each state in 
each time period to create an index of lobbying regulation with a possible 
range of 0 to 18.
 
Thus, I have developed an index to measure state lobbying regulation 
that has face validity and that can be replicated over time. This index also is 
validated by comparison to another measure of lobbying regulation that was 
developed for a single period of time. My index at the earliest time period 
(1990–91) correlates with Opheim’s (1991) measure at 0.84 (p < 0.01).3 The 
correlation between my index in 1990–91 and in 2003 is 0.46 (p < 0.01), 
suggesting that my index reﬂects the changes in regulation between these 
periods.4 Furthermore, the Cronbach’s alpha for the 17 components of my 
index that went into the 2003 measure was 0.71, also indicating the index 
is reliable.5 Taken together, these diagnostics increase my conﬁdence in my 
index’s validity and reliability.
an examination of lobbying regulation
Table 1 shows how state lobbying regulation has changed from 1990 to 2003. 
The states are ordered in the table from those with the strictest regulations in 
the 2003 time period to those with the least strict regulations. The relevant 
regulation score is reported for each state in each time period. In the 1990–91 
time period, the index ranged from 1 to 14 with a mean of 6.54 and a stan-

restrictive lobbying laws, followed by Connecticut (13) and Maryland (13). 
In 2003, the index ranged from 1 to 17 with a mean of 10.34 and a standard 
deviation of 3.15. South Carolina (17), Arkansas (15), Maine (15), Texas 
(15), Washington (15), California (14), and Kentucky (14) had the strictest

The data suggest that, on average, the states have increased their regula-
tion of lobbyists since 1990. The last column of Table 1 shows the change 
from the earliest to the most recent period. Some states had especially large 
jumps in lobbying regulation restrictiveness; South Carolina’s laws increased 
in stringency by 750 percent, Georgia’s by 700 percent, and South Dakota’s 
by 600 percent. South Carolina not only had the largest percent change, but it 
also moved from nearly the least to the most restrictive state in the nation in 
lobbying regulation. Of course, some caution should be used when examining 
these percentage changes, as some of the larger increases in lobbying regula-
tion still leave the state ranked near the middle when compared to the other 
states. Because Georgia and South Dakota had the least restrictive lobbying 
regulation in 1990–91, their large percentage increases only put them near 
the mean for all states in 2003.
 
Another way of looking at changes in these regulations is to consider the 
relative rankings of the states from the earliest to the latest time period. Thir-
teen states moved up in the rankings by ten spots or more. South Carolina’s 
ranking increased the most, moving from 43rd in 1990–91 to 1st in 2003, 
according to my index, which is a 42–spot jump. Kentucky moved up 32 
spots from 38th to 6th, and Utah moved up 20 spots from 28th to 8th. Mis-
sissippi, Vermont, and Ohio each moved up 19 spots. On the other hand, 
nine states dropped 10 or more spots in their lobbying regulation rankings. 
Notably, Virginia, which had been ranked 8th in 1990–91 was ranked 44th 
in 2002, a 36–point slide. Indiana, which had the 5th most stringent lobby-
ing regulation in 1990–91 was ranked 37th in 2003, a decline of 32 spots. 
Additionally, Nebraska, Iowa, Oklahoma, and North Dakota each dropped 
at least 16 spots. Thus, given the changes that have occurred in lobbying and 
ethics laws in many statehouses, existing measures of lobbying stringency, 
such as the ones constructed by Opheim (1991) and Brinig, Holcombe, and 
Schwartzstein (1993), are now dated.6 Many states, such as South Carolina 
and Kentucky, adopted more stringent lobbying laws in the 1990s follow-
ing embarrassing scandals (Ensign 1997). Rosenson (2003) has also shown 
changes among the states in the authorization of state ethics commissions.

while others list a number of statutes followed by a vague statement that 
violations of the previous section are unlawful. States may list penalties for 
lobbying regulation violation in sections under headings dealing with the 
legislature, lobbyists, or general governmental ethics. To assess these penalties, 
I conducted a thorough examination of the state codes based on a number 
of criteria, including whether a violation of any of the above mentioned 
gift bans, prohibited activities, or disclosure requirements was a felony or 
a misdemeanor.7 I also considered penalties for failure to ﬁle a report with 
the appropriate state agency, failure to register, or ﬁling a false statement. 
States that speciﬁed larger ﬁnes or harsher prison sentences for violations 
were considered to have stricter regulations than those with smaller or no 
ﬁnes or lesser or no prison sentences. While I could not retroactively assess 
state statutes for lobbying regulation penalties in previous periods, I noted 
state lobbying regulation penalty stringency in the most recent period based 
on the above considerations after three examinations of their 2003 statutes.8 
The states were scored 1 to 4, with 1 indicating the weakest penalties and 4 
the strictest.
 
The state scores for penalties in 2003 for violating lobbying regulations 
are found in Table 1 (2nd column from right). I added these values to the 
2003 measure of regulation.9 States like South Carolina, Kentucky, and New 
York had high stringency scores and severe penalties for violating the law. On 
the other end of the index, North Dakota and Wyoming had low regulation 
scores and lenient penalties for violating their already weak laws.
 
Why have some states altered their lobbying regulations since 1990, 
changing the state rankings on this policy?10 According to Goodman and his 
colleagues (1996), scandals and media coverage may result in state legislators 
initiating reforms of their state’s ethics and lobbying laws. Kentucky, which 
on my index moved up 32 spots from 1990–91 to 2003 and now has the sixth 
most restrictive lobbying regulations, had its share of scandals in this period, 
and legislators have been forced to adapt to stricter regulations. In the early 
1990s, a number of lawmakers and lobbyists in Kentucky were imprisoned 
for corruption, including former state House Speaker, Don Blandford (Ensign 
1997). South Carolina legislators were involved in several scandals in the 
1990s, including one in which some state legislators were caught on camera 
selling their votes (Grimm 2003). As a result, South Carolina now has the 
most stringent lobbying regulation in the country, and it had the third larg-
est rise in state rankings between 1990–91 and 2003, moving up 28 spots 
on my index. New York and Ohio also have more stringent regulation of

It is also possible that legislators could weaken ethics laws once enacted. 
States like Virginia and Indiana weakened their lobbying regulation between 
1990–91 and 2003. This suggests that once media coverage and public salien-
cy over a scandal has waned, legislators may quietly backslide on regulations. 
Wisconsin and Minnesota are also somewhat unique in that they have rela-
tively stringent lobbying regulation despite an absence of major scandals in 
recent years. Their regulations have been restrictive throughout the study 
period. Legislators in these states (or their constituents) may have more 
ethical views of proper legislative behavior than in other states. It is also pos-
sible that some less restrictive states may not have had the need to regulate 
lobbying more because their legislators have been relatively well-behaved. 
North Dakota may be an example of this.
conclusion
My index provides political scientists with a replicable measure of lobbying 
regulation in the states that can be used to assess a number of hypotheses of 
legislative and interest group behavior and policymaking. But, an important 
practical question is whether these regulations actually alter behavior. It 
seems likely that these laws would prevent bribery or other blatant unethical 
behaviors due to their punitive effects, but the vigor of their enforcement 
also affects this, and my index does not take this into account. When these 
laws are enacted following scandals, media exposure may help serve to limit 
subsequent violations. However, at a more basic level, we have yet to deter-
mine how these laws alter the interpersonal relationships between lobbyists 
and legislators. That is, do lobbyists adjust their strategies in dealing with 
legislators because of state regulation of their activities? And, if they do, how 
do their strategies change? Those questions beg further research and are 
important for both practical and theoretical reasons.
