Lobbying regulation in the states revisited: What are
we trying to measure, and how do we measure it?
Adam J. Newmark1
 Macmillan Publishers Ltd 2017
Abstract The states regulate lobbying based on how they deﬁne lobbyists and
interest groups, by requiring reporting and disclosure of certain activities, and by
prohibiting a number of activities between lobbyists and government ofﬁcials.
These laws are designed to provide accountability and to alter certain political
behaviors. For decades, scholars have studied these laws, while a number of good
government groups have created their own measures assessing lobby regulations.
The result is a multitude of measures that often assess very different constructs. This
article creates an updated measure of lobbying regulation and compares this mea-
sure to previous and current, alternative measures.
Keywords Lobbyists  Lobbying regulation in the US states  Interest groups
Introduction
For over a quarter of a century, political scientists have studied the factors that have
led to lobbying regulations and general ethics laws (Opheim 1991; Rosenson 2005;
Ozymy 2013), how we should measure these laws (Newmark 2005), the effects of
these laws on interest communities (Lowery and Gray 1997; Gray and Lowery
1998; Strickland 2014), the effects of these laws on policy (Ozymy 2010), and the
relationship between these laws and transparency (Holman and Luneburg 2012) and
public opinion (Flavin 2015). In essence, while we have advanced our understand-
ing about lobbying regulation, we still have variation in how we measure these laws.
Speciﬁcally, we may have validity and reliability issues in how we measure
lobbying laws, how different organizations assess lobbying laws, and how we make

whether these measures are appropriate to answer the research questions we ask.
Accordingly, I address several questions in this article. First, how have political
scientists and various organizations examined lobbying regulations in recent years?
Second, how can we construct a valid measure of lobbying regulation? Third, how
have these laws changed over the past decade? Answers to these questions should
provide us with a better understanding of how and why states regulate lobbying, and
whether or not it matters in altering political behaviors.
Literature
For nearly three decades, scholars have studied lobbying regulations in a number of
different ways. Opheim (1991) developed a measure of lobbying regulation, organized
around how the states deﬁned lobbying, disclosure requirements, and enforcement in
order to determine why some states had strict regulation of lobbying behavior, and
others did not. Culture, for example, had an inﬂuence on the passage of regulations
designed to limit political behaviors (Opheim 1991; Rosenson 2005; Ozymy 2013);
party competition and legislative staff also explained the stringency of lobbying laws
(Opheim 1991). Some scholars have examined how lobbying laws affect interest
communities. Brinig et al. (1993) argued that lobbying regulation inhibited the number
of lobbyists in the states because registration requirements served as a barrier to entry.
On the other hand, Hunter et al. (1991) and Lowery and Gray (1997) found that
regulations did not have a bearing on the number of interests registered in the states.
Gray and Lowery (1998) also found that these regulations did not restrict the diversity of
interests within interest systems. Newmark (2005) examined lobbying regulation over
time with the goal of creating a replicable measure, which has been used in a number of
other studies of lobbying (Ozymy 2010, 2013; Strickland 2014; Flavin 2015).
Perhaps the most obvious question is whether lobbying regulations can alter
political behaviors, something Ozymy (2010) examined, ﬁnding that these laws
reduced interest group inﬂuence. Moreover, this research suggests that the
relationship is dynamic, whereby changes in lobbying regulation should result in
changes in interest group inﬂuence. Flavin’s (2015) novel work on lobbying laws
and political representation found that stricter regulations appeared to encourage
political equality in the states, perhaps by limiting those with undue inﬂuence.
A number of recent works have focused on lobbying across nations (Hogan et al.
2008; Mihut 2008; Holman 2008). Holman and Luneburg (2012) examined
lobbying regulation cross-nationally, focusing on transparency as a means of
regulating lobbying behavior. Echoing conclusions by Mihut (2008), they found that
lobbying regulation in Europe is becoming more like the regulation in the USA.
Some studies have addressed the restriction of lobbyists more obliquely. That is,
by addressing restrictions on other aspects of state politics, these studies examine

How to regulate lobbying
Deﬁnitions of lobbyists and registration requirements
The states attempt to regulate lobbying behavior in a number of ways. To begin, states
deﬁne which individuals must register to lobby, and what entities must register with
the relevant government agency. These deﬁnitions differentiate a lobbyist from a
citizen who contacts lawmakers or other government ofﬁcials. The states may deﬁne a
lobbyist based on a set amount of time devoted to the job or expenditures of a certain
amount in their lobbying efforts. Further, the states vary in deﬁning ‘legislative
liaisons’—who are typically employed by executive branch agencies or other
governments—as lobbyists who represent their governmental units before the
legislature. Much of the research on the deﬁnition of lobbying focuses on whether or
not lobbying registration requirements have a bearing on the density (or number) of
interest groups. Some scholars suggest that more restrictive lobbying laws limit the
number of interests in the state (Brinig et al. 1993), while other research suggests that
density (Hunter et al. 1991; Lowery and Gray 1997) and diversity (Gray and Lowery
1998) are unaffected by registration requirements. The logic is that a small ﬁling fee
and routine paperwork will not inhibit groups from registering to lobby that have a
vested interest before the legislature. While these deﬁnitions may not curtail numbers
and diversity, they do have a direct bearing on which organizations and lobbyists are
restricted in their lobbying behaviors. Thus, it is still a form of regulation, and more
encompassing deﬁnitions of lobbyists and interest groups are indications that the
states are attempting to regulate lobbying to include a greater number of individuals
and organizations. States that include fewer individuals and organization in their
deﬁnitions may exclude restrictions on some would-be lobbyists.
Reporting/disclosure requirements
Along with the deﬁnitions of who must register is the information collected when
registration takes place and information that must be disclosed at various intervals.
The states, to varying extents, require the disclosure of activities ranging from money
spent on lobbying, contributions given to candidates, or gifts provided. Disclosure
often includes compensation lobbyists receive or expenditures they make in their
lobbying efforts. The type of itemization required varies from state to state, and some
states are more detailed in their requirements than others. In Idaho, lobbyists have
monthly reports during the legislative session on their total expenses, as well as

requires information on the recipient, the nature of, and the amount of the

and Luneburg 2012) or at least the perception of it. Theoretically, this transparency
serves as a restriction on lobbyists because lawmakers may be hesitant to accept
money or gifts from lobbyists representing controversial groups.
Prohibited activities
Lobbying laws also aim to restrict certain actions ranging from vote buying to providing
gifts of any value at all. Vote buying is of course illegal in all 50 states, but laws are also
designed to limit less explicit offerings that involve some potential exchange for favors.
Some states set monetary limits on the amount that can be spent on a lawmaker; some
states even limit providing cups of coffee to lawmakers. Revolving door laws are
designed to restrict lawmakers from becoming lobbyists without a cooling-off period
(LaPira et al. 2014). These laws require that lawmakers wait a set amount of time—
usually a year or two—before they can utilize their former positions and relationships,
which could give them an advantage in lobbying. Florida and Iowa require a two-year
cooling-off period before former lawmakers can become lobbyists, while states
including Texas, Nebraska, and Vermont do not require a wait at all.3
Penalties and enforcement
Perhaps the most difﬁcult part of regulating lobbyists, and studying these laws by
political scientists, involves the penalties for violating a state’s lobbying laws. Some
states provide very clear laws that lay out what constitutes violations and what the
penalties are for violations, while others are far less clear (Newmark 2005). Even
more difﬁcult to assess is the willingness and ability to enforce the law. Prosecutors
and attorneys general may lack the necessary time and resources to pursue alleged
transgressions. Additionally, enforcement agents may be reluctant to target ofﬁcials
with whom they may have had long-standing relationships (Vaughan and Newmark
2008; Newmark and Vaughan 2014).

In many ways, these different organizations have different motivations for
assessing state efforts to restrict lobbying activities. While there are some
similarities in these measures, they appear to measure different constructs.

Constructing an updated measure
The purpose here is to update a theoretically sound, valid, and replicable measure of
lobbying regulation. The natural starting place would be the Council of State
Governments’ The Book of the State, but the data have not been published since
2005. The Book of the States’ data have been used in a number of studies (cf.
Newmark 2005), and the categories that comprise these data make theoretical sense
in terms of assessing regulation. The categories capture who must register to lobby,
what activities lobbyists can and cannot do, and what information must be disclosed.
Accordingly, the best strategy is to reproduce the same data produced in the Book
of the States through an examination of state statutes, constitutional provisions
where applicable, and review of materials from relevant departments or agencies

have been used in the past and evaluated by a number of scholars (Opheim 1991;

As noted, the states vary in the scope with which they deﬁne lobbying, so I ﬁrst
assess how broad or narrow the deﬁnitions are in the states. These components are
designed to ascertain who is required to register to lobby and, therefore, who is
regulated by the state when contacting government ofﬁcials in an advocacy
capacity. Deﬁnitions include whether the following necessitate registering as a
lobbyist: legislative lobbying, administrative agency lobbying, elective ofﬁcials as
lobbyists, public employees as lobbyists, and whether there are compensation,
expenditure, and time standards. All 50 states require registration for lobbying the
legislature, and most states require it for lobbying administrative agencies. Some
states require elected ofﬁcials and public appointees to register if they engage in
certain lobbying-related behaviors. The states may also stipulate that persons are
deﬁned as lobbyists if they receive a certain amount of money in exchange for
lobbying (compensation standards), if they spend a certain amount of money in
lobbying (expenditure standards), or if they devote a certain amount of time in their
lobbying efforts (time standards). Each of these is coded 1 if the state includes the
provision in its deﬁnition of a lobbyist and 0 otherwise. Thus, some states regulate a
greater number of potential lobbyists because they have more inclusive deﬁnitions.
Second, I examine which lobbying activities and behaviors are prohibited in the
states. Prohibited activities include: if lobbyists are prohibited from making
campaign contributions at any time; if they are prohibited from making campaign
contributions during the legislative sessions; solicitations by ofﬁcials or employees
for contributions or gifts; contingent compensation; and revolving door restrictions.
These ﬁrst three are generally self-explanatory. Contingent compensation refers to
restrictions on lobbyists receiving pay only if they achieve some lobbying-related
objective. Revolving door laws prohibit lawmakers from becoming lobbyists
without a waiting period before they can lobby. Each of these components gives an
indication of how far the state goes in restricting certain activities; each is coded 1/0
depending on if the state prohibits a given activity.
Third are Registration and reporting/disclosure requirements, which include
whether the following activities require reporting or disclosure: seeking to inﬂuence
legislation or administrative action, expenditures beneﬁtting public ofﬁcials or
employees, compensation received (broken down by employer), total compensa-
tions received, categories of expenditures, total expenditures toward lobbying, and

categories was given a 1 if the state included the provision and a 0 if it did not, and
the categories were then summed to create an index assessing the level of lobbying
regulation in each state.

Theoretically, the index might have dimensions in the categories above such as
one based on deﬁnitions, one based on reporting/disclosure requirements, and one
based on prohibited activities. Factor analysis reveals that there are likely multiple
dimensions in state lobbying regulations, and there are between four and six factors
depending on the factor model utilized and the eigenvalue cut-points. ‘‘Appendix’’
(Table 3) contains factor loadings for a four-factor model, which is orthogonally
rotated given the lack of low correlations among each factor. There appears to be a
factor pertaining to deﬁnitions that includes administrative agency lobbying,
compensation standards, expenditure standards, and time standards. This factor
also includes high loadings for two disclosure requirements: seeking to inﬂuence
legislative/administrative action and contributions received from other for lobbying
purposes. A second factor includes prohibited activities such as making campaign
contributions at any time, contingent compensation, and revolving door restrictions,
as well as disclosure requirements of total compensation received and compensation
received (broken down by employer). A third factor has several components with
high loadings including the prohibited activity of solicitation by ofﬁcials or
employees for contributions or gifts and discloser requirements of total expenditures
and categories of expenditures. Finally, a fourth factor is largely based on
deﬁnitional components with high loadings for elected ofﬁcials as lobbyists, public
employees as lobbyists, and a high negative loading for administrative agency
lobbying. While the data do not organize neatly along a preconceived structure, the
ﬁrst and fourth factors are primarily based on deﬁnitions, the second is based on
prohibited activities, and the third is based largely on disclosure. I further address
the dimensionality of lobby regulation below.

the past? The new measure correlates with the Sunlight Foundation score at 0.4.
Since the new measure is broader substantively and has greater variance, it is not
surprising that there is a moderate relationship between the two measures. At the
bottom of the new rankings, Florida, South Dakota, Nevada, North Dakota, and
Wyoming all also have rankings of D or F, according to the Sunlight Foundation.
The results are more varied at the top of the rankings. Of the seven highest ranked
states on the new measure, three received A’s, one received a B, two received C’s,
and one received a D from the Sunlight Foundation.
The new measure correlates with the CPI measure at 0.52, which is not surprising
given some of the commonalities across the two measures. However, the new index
is only weakly correlated with the PRI accessibility index (r = 0.27), and it is
generally unrelated to the PRI disclosure index. Given that the PRI measure is
unrelated to the new measure and the CPI measure, it is safe to say that it is
measuring something different.
Discussion and conclusion
Some states have moved up notably in the rankings. Arizona is an interesting case in
that the state ranked very low on the 2003 ranking from the Book of the States, yet
ranks higher on the new measure. While Arizona scores relatively high when it
comes to the deﬁnition and prohibited activity components and high on the
disclosure score, there are limitations in disclosure laws in terms of determining the
recipients of monetary contributions (Price 2015).10 Thus, it is possible to have what
appears to be stringent disclosure requirements in reporting what money is spent by
lobbyists, while in reality, those requirements may contain gaps in requiring key
information such as where the money is spent.
Additionally, Virginia, Illinois, and North Carolina all moved from relatively low
rankings in 2003 to relatively high rankings in 2015. In cases like these, states
may increase the stringency of their lobbying laws following some scandal
involving lobbyists and lawmakers (cf. Ozymy 2013). Such was the case in states
like South Carolina and Kentucky prior to the 2003 rankings (Newmark 2005).
Virginia appears to have responded to scandals involving former governor Bob
McDonnell, though many of the changes to their lobbying laws did not go into
effect until 2016 (Rohlﬁng 2015).11 While North Carolina has had its share of
political scandals, including scandals involving the former Secretary of Agriculture
and the former House Speaker, the state appears to have adopted more rigorous
lobbying laws independently of these scandals, rather than as a response to them.
Thus, the states will likely continue to alter their lobbying laws to adjust to
competing political pressures, address potentially embarrassing political scandals,

or based on what is politically beneﬁcial. While good government groups may
desire stricter lobbying laws, many interest groups, contract ﬁrms, and those with
access would prefer fewer restrictions on lobbying. And lawmakers have a
disincentive to restrict potential beneﬁts from lobbyists unless there is signiﬁcant
public demand.
The measure constructed here, like previous measures of lobbying regulation, is
made up of components involving deﬁnitions, reporting/disclosure requirements,
and prohibited activities. But the analysis conducted here does not indicate three
tidy factors. Instead, the data are structured partly along these dimensions, but with
notable differences. This suggests that lobbying regulations may not fall into neat
categories where states simply decide how narrowly or broadly to craft their
deﬁnitions of lobbying. It is not as simple as having a legislature to decide how
much transparency they want and then create laws that require the corresponding
level of disclosure. Instead, it may be the case that states that require a great deal of
disclosure may not feel the need to prohibit as many activities, or having many
prohibited activities obviates the need for strict reporting requirements. New
Hampshire, Nebraska, and Hawaii are examples of the former, scoring seven out of
seven on the disclosure metric, while scoring much lower on the prohibited activity
metric (see Table 2). As further evidence of this assertion, the factor analysis
revealed very low correlations among the different factors.
It is also possible that states with broad ethics requirements may not need as
many separate laws directed at lobbyists. The types of regulations mentioned above
all try to limit lobbyists directly, but it is also possible to restrict lobbying indirectly
by restricting activities of the government ofﬁcials who are lobbied. That is, the
examples above limit lobbying by disrupting the supply—gifts, contributions, or
activities—from the lobbyists. But lobbying can also be restricted by limiting what
lawmakers or other governmental ofﬁcials can accept. So, in place of, or in addition
to restricting lobbyists, government ofﬁcials can be restricted in their behaviors and
in what beneﬁts they can receive from lobbyists. Thus, lobbyists can be constrained
indirectly by disrupting the demand—in this case, the ability of government ofﬁcials
to accept beneﬁts from lobbyists.
Another consideration is that different measures, by design, measure different
things. The Sunlight Foundation measure focuses on transparency, while the
measure developed in this article is much broader in scope. The Center for Public
Integrity also grades the states, focusing narrowly on lobbying disclosure, but it also
has measures that assess overall accountability and integrity for the state, as well as
electoral oversight, ethics enforcement, and internal auditing (among others).12
Thus, it is important to ﬁrst consider what the measure is actually assessing. It is
likely that different measures may be explained by a different set of factors. And,
different measures may affect political behaviors somewhat differently.
Accordingly, scholars should not blindly incorporate measures of lobbying
regulation without ﬁrst considering the theoretical justiﬁcation for a given
restriction or set of restrictions. So, prohibited activities should inﬂuence what
lobbyists can and cannot do, while disclosure requirements may have a much lesser

inﬂuence on behaviors. While it is certainly possible that disclosure requirements
may limit the occasional contribution from a hate group, this is of course a very
uncommon issue, and therefore, disclosure requirements may have limited inﬂuence
on behaviors. But the goal of disclosure requirements is to promote transparency,
rather than to primarily inﬂuence the actions of political actors. Scholars would be
wise to consider these and other goals and incorporate a theoretically appropriate
measure of lobbying regulation based on the nature of the research and the type of
activity under study. The updated measure is useful if the goal is to understand
broadly how the states regulate lobbying and how these laws regulate general
lobbying behavior. There may be cases, however, where more speciﬁc constituent
parts of the measure would better suit the narrow activities under study. In such
cases, the deﬁnition, prohibited activity, or disclosure components may be most
appropriate. These observations would likely apply to studying the effect of
lobbying laws at the national level, or even in other countries, as well as American
states.
